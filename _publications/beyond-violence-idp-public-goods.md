title: "Beyond Violence: Manipulating Internal Displacement Through Selective Public Goods Provision During Civil War"
authors: "Zhejun Qiu"
abstract: "The migration patterns of civilians are a crucial determinant of battles on the frontline and conflict spillover to the backline. Civilians in conflict zones serve as a recruitment pool for rebels, while relocation and internal migration to the backline can export instability to previously peaceful regions. Governments therefore face a tradeoff between weakening rebels in contested areas and preventing conflict from spreading, and rebels must balance conscription and governance to ensure that enough civilians stay. Current research provides insights on civil war actors' regulation of migration through violence, yet nonviolent tools such as wartime governance, displacement aid, and protection remain understudied. This paper develops a formal model with a government, a rebel group, and a continuous distribution of civilians to examine when wartime actors provide aid and governance to civilians in contested areas, and how effective migration-management aid shapes wartime internal migration. The equilibrium shows that when civilians’ cost of war is high, governments and rebels may collude in migration control by increasing aid on the frontline. Moreover, when the backline is highly unstable, the government has an incentive to aid rebel supporters to offset the contagion of conflict, even if this strengthens the rebel. Using the International Organization for Migration’s camp assessment panel surveys (Rounds 24–32) in Ethiopia during the Tigray War (2020–2023), I provide empirical evidence consistent with the model’s predictions."
